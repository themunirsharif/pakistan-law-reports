PLJ 2014 Cr.C. (Lahore) 862

High Court retains power to suspend sentence pending Supreme Court appeal, regardless of when leave was granted

CourtLahore High Court, Multan
Case No.Crl. Misc. Nos. 84-M, 625-M, 450-M, 937-M of 2013 and 127-M, 40-M, 81-M of 2014
Date decided24 June 2014
BenchMuhammad Qasim Khan and Muhammad Tariq Abbasi, JJ.
Criminal & Bail

Headnote

Criminal Procedure Code, 1898 — S. 426(2B) — Suspension of sentence pending appeal — Special leave to appeal granted by the Supreme Court is a distinct proceeding from a High Court's certificate of fitness for appeal — A High Court may suspend sentence and release a convict on bail under S. 426(2B) once the Supreme Court grants leave to appeal against a sentence the High Court imposed or maintained, regardless of whether leave was granted at the same time as the High Court's own order.

Judgment

Muhammad Tariq Abbasi, J. — This single order decides seven connected petitions (Talib Hussain v. The State, Amjad Farooq v. The State, Anwaar v. The State, Muhammad Safdar v. The State, Qazafi v. The State, Muhammad Bilal v. The State, and Muhammad Aslam v. The State), all filed under Section 426(2B) Cr.P.C. seeking suspension of sentence after the Supreme Court of Pakistan had granted leave to appeal in each case.

The Deputy Prosecutor General raised a preliminary objection: that an application under Section 426(2B) is only competent before the High Court if the High Court itself, at the time of deciding the appeal, imposed or maintained the sentence and simultaneously granted leave to appeal to the Supreme Court, not where leave was granted separately, afterward, by the Supreme Court itself.

History of Section 426(2B)

The provision traces back to the second amendment to the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898 (Act IV of 1946), prompted directly by the Privy Council's ruling in Lala Jairam Das and others v. Emperor (A.I.R. (32) 1945 P.C. 94). There, the Privy Council held that a High Court in India had no power to grant bail to a convict who had been given special leave to appeal to His Majesty in Council, while acknowledging that such a power "would be a proper and useful power to vest in a High Court," achievable only through legislation, not judicial interpretation.

Parliament responded by inserting sub-section (2B) into Section 426, giving High Courts exactly that power once special leave had been granted.

Tracing the constitutional lineage

The Court traced how the relevant appellate-jurisdiction provision evolved across Pakistan's constitutional history, from Articles 158-160 of the 1956 Constitution, through Article 58 of the 1962 Constitution (which merged those three articles and renamed "Special Leave to Appeal" to simply "Leave to Appeal"), to Article 186 of the 1972 interim constitution, and finally to Article 185 of the 1973 Constitution presently in force.

Throughout every version, one distinction survived unchanged: a certificate that a case is fit for appeal is something a High Court issues; leave to appeal (formerly "special leave") is something only the Supreme Court can grant. No law in Pakistan gives a High Court authority to itself grant leave to appeal, that remains the Supreme Court's exclusive constitutional prerogative.

Holding

Because the Supreme Court alone grants leave to appeal, and a High Court's role is limited to certifying fitness for appeal, the Deputy Prosecutor General's objection could not stand. Section 426(2B) does not require that leave be granted at the same moment the High Court disposes of the appeal; it applies whenever a convicted person has been granted leave to appeal by the Supreme Court against a sentence the High Court imposed or maintained, at any point pending that appeal.

The objection was overruled, and all seven petitions were remitted to the respective Benches for decision on merits.
